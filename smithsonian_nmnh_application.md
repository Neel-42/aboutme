# Smithsonian National Museum of Natural History Internship Application

## Why are you interested in interning at the National Museum of Natural History?

For the past two years, I have volunteered as a youth teaching assistant in Smithsonian summer camps, and I have watched how museums transform the way young people understand science, history, and their place in the world. During those experiences, I have taken students to the National Museum of Natural History multiple times, and each visit revealed something new: how a well-designed exhibit can make complex concepts accessible, how interactive displays spark curiosity, and how museums can bridge the gap between abstract knowledge and lived experience.

What fascinates me most is the potential for museums to serve as spaces where technology—especially AI—can enhance learning without replacing the wonder of discovery. I am particularly interested in how AI tools could personalize museum experiences, making natural history more engaging for diverse learners while maintaining the authenticity and rigor that make museums trusted institutions. As someone who works as a peer tutor helping students learn science, I have seen firsthand how different learners need different approaches. The same principle applies to museum visitors: a middle schooler exploring fossils needs a different entry point than a high school student studying climate change, yet both deserve access to the same depth of knowledge. An internship at NMNH would let me explore how emerging technologies can make museum education more inclusive, personalized, and effective—especially for young people who might not see themselves reflected in traditional science education.

## How will this experience help you achieve your personal/academic goals?

My long-term goal is to research how AI affects society and how we can harness it for good—work that sits at the intersection of economics, data science, and ethics. This internship would be invaluable because museums are precisely the kind of public-facing institution where AI's social impact becomes visible: How do visitors interact with AI-powered exhibits? Do personalized recommendations increase engagement or create filter bubbles? Can AI make museum content more accessible to underserved communities, or does it inadvertently widen digital divides?

Through my current internship at UVA's School of Data Science working on health economics, I have learned to analyze how technology interventions affect different populations. At NMNH, I would apply similar analytical thinking to museum education, learning how to assess whether AI tools genuinely improve learning outcomes or simply create the appearance of innovation. This experience would also strengthen my understanding of how public institutions balance technological advancement with educational mission—a critical skill for someone planning to study ethics alongside data science.

Academically, this internship would complement my coursework at Brown on data science and food/migration by giving me hands-on experience in a different domain: education and public engagement. It would also prepare me for undergraduate research by teaching me how to design studies that measure real-world impact, not just technical performance. Most importantly, it would help me develop the interdisciplinary perspective I need to eventually work on AI policy and ethics—understanding not just what technology can do, but what it should do, and for whom.

## Describe an experience where your unique perspective contributed to a project or activity.

During my second year volunteering as a youth teaching assistant at Smithsonian summer camps, I noticed that some students were struggling to connect with certain exhibits—not because the content was too difficult, but because the presentation didn't match their learning styles. One student, in particular, was fascinated by ocean ecosystems but found the traditional text-heavy panels overwhelming. Drawing on my experience as a peer tutor, where I have learned to adapt explanations for different learners, I suggested we create a simple interactive activity: students would "become" different marine animals and explain their roles in the ecosystem to each other.

What started as a small adaptation became something larger. The camp director asked me to help design similar activities for other groups, and I began thinking about how technology could scale this kind of personalization. I proposed using simple digital tools (like QR codes linking to short videos or interactive quizzes) that could give visitors multiple entry points into the same content. My perspective—combining hands-on teaching experience with an interest in how technology can enhance (not replace) human learning—helped the team think differently about accessibility.

That experience taught me that my unique value comes from bridging domains: understanding both the educational needs of young learners and the potential of emerging technologies. It also reinforced my belief that effective innovation requires listening to the people who will actually use the tools, not just building what seems technically impressive. This is exactly the kind of thinking I hope to bring to NMNH—using technology thoughtfully to make museum experiences more inclusive and engaging.

I bring several strengths that would make me effective at NMNH. I am not interested in AI simply because it is new; I am interested in understanding when and how it genuinely improves outcomes, especially for underserved communities. This perspective would help me contribute thoughtfully to projects exploring how museums can use emerging technologies responsibly.

Finally, I am comfortable working both independently and collaboratively, and I am eager to learn from museum professionals, researchers, and other interns. I see this internship as an opportunity to grow, not just to demonstrate what I already know.
